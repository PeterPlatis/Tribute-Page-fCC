The myth of the Odyssey

  • Odysseus and his legion embarked from Troy on twelve ships. The calm sea facilitated their voyage, carrying them far from shore. After several days, they spotted land. Eurylochus, Odysseus's second-in-command, urged him to drop anchor, go ashore, and pillage the city with the promise of safety.

    Observing the ships dropping anchor and warriors disembarking, the Ciconians, local inhabitants, fled to the nearby mountains. Odysseus and his men ransacked the deserted city. However, Odysseus struggled to rally his men back to the ships; they indulged in a lavish feast, accompanied by wine flowing freely, before falling asleep on the shore.

    At dawn, the Ciconians, reinforced by their fierce allies, launched a counterattack, slaughtering many of the warriors. Odysseus and his surviving men hastily retreated to their ships, having suffered heavy losses. Odysseus lamented his decision to heed Eurylochus's advice, which led to the loss of valuable comrades. Tempers flared as Odysseus and Eurylochus quarreled, but their fellow warriors intervened, restoring peace among them.

  • Navigating towards the south, Odysseus and his crew were swept off course, eventually finding themselves near the territory of the Lotus-Eaters. As Odysseus ventured through the land, some of his companions interacted with the locals and indulged in the consumption of the native lotus plants. Subsequently, a fog of intoxication descended upon them, lulling the men into a deep slumber.

    The lotus flowers they ingested possessed narcotic properties, causing them to lose all recollection of their families and homeland. Enthralled by the effects, these men expressed a desire to remain on the island indefinitely, reveling in the consumption of lotus. Resolutely, Odysseus and a few others had to physically transport them back to the ship. Swiftly, they embarked and, upon awakening, the affected men had to be restrained to the ship's masts to prevent them from leaping into the sea and returning to the shore in pursuit of more lotus, to which they had become ensnared.

  • Following many weeks of uneventful sailing, the warriors stumbled upon an unfamiliar land. Odysseus and a small group of his men ventured ashore to explore.

    Just a brief walk from their ships led them to the mouth of an immense cave. Intrigued, the warriors entered and discovered it to be the dwelling of a colossal being. Deeper within, they encountered herds of sheep and, driven by hunger, they slaughtered some and feasted. Unbeknownst to them, this cave belonged to Polyphemus the Cyclops, and the land was the domain of these gigantic beings.

    Upon returning to the cave, Polyphemus sealed the entrance with a massive rock, trapping Odysseus and his men inside. Desperate, they found themselves confronted by the Cyclops, even more imposing than the boulder blocking their escape. Polyphemus demanded to know their identity and purpose. Odysseus, withholding the truth, claimed they were lost sailors seeking provisions.

    Infuriated by the loss of his sheep, Polyphemus refused to let them leave, instead devouring two of Odysseus's men daily. Unable to tolerate this brutality, Odysseus devised a plan. Offering Polyphemus potent wine, he waited until the Cyclops fell into a drunken slumber. Seizing the opportunity, Odysseus and his comrades blinded Polyphemus with a red-hot poker.

    Awakened in agony, Polyphemus cried out for revenge, but Odysseus cunningly claimed his name was "Nobody." Confused and in pain, Polyphemus attracted the attention of his fellow Cyclopes, who mocked him when he claimed "Nobody" had blinded him.

    The next morning, Odysseus and his men concealed themselves beneath the sheep and escaped as Polyphemus let his flock graze. As they fled to their ships, Odysseus, unable to resist, taunted Polyphemus, revealing his identity. Enraged and blinded, Polyphemus hurled a rock towards Odysseus's voice, narrowly missing the ship. Calling upon his father, Poseidon, for vengeance, Polyphemus cursed Odysseus, making him an enemy of the sea-god.

  • As Odysseus fled the realm of the Cyclopes, his ships drew near Aeolia, the abode of Aeolus, the deity of the winds. Aeolus wielded authority over the winds that traversed both sea and land. Upon learning of Odysseus' quest for home, Aeolus bestowed upon him a sack brimming with winds, destined to guide him safely homeward. Odysseus embarked once more upon the vast expanse of the sea, spending countless nights vigilant, safeguarding the precious sack. Yet, in a moment of exhaustion, he succumbed to sleep.

    Intrigue seized a pair of his comrades, eager to uncover the mystery their leader guarded so zealously. Seizing the opportune moment as Odysseus slumbered, and with Ithaca's shores within reach, the two sailors breached the sack's confines. The winds, liberated from their captivity, unleashed a tempest of fury, propelling the ships backward. Startled awake by the unsettling motion of the vessel, Odysseus found himself once more in Aeolia. This time, Aeolus, rueful of the outcome, withheld the gift of the winds. With a heavy heart, Odysseus embarked once more on the daunting voyage to Ithaca.

  • Emerging from the shroud of night, an island materialized on the horizon. This was Telepylos, its formidable cliffs serving as natural defenses, offering only a single narrow inlet. Each vessel navigated through this passage into the tranquil harbor embraced by cliffs, save for Odysseus, who, inexplicably, moored his ship amidst the tumultuous waters beyond.

    Venturing ashore, two warriors started exploring, encountering a maiden who led them to her father. As they approached the fortress, they saw a colossal woman who summoned her spouse. Bursting forth was a towering man, her husband, seizing one of the men and consuming him whole. The other fled for his life, pursued relentlessly by the entire race of giants dwelling in the land. At the harbor, Odysseus' crew sought refuge, yet the giants obliterated their vessels with colossal stones, impaling the sailors alive. Only Odysseus, having anchored his ship outside the island, narrowly escaped with a handful of sailors.

  • Barely escaping peril, Odysseus and his crew find refuge on Aeaea, the domain of Circe, a formidable enchantress and sorceress of great power. Unbeknownst to the men, Circe had foreseen their arrival through her potent magic. Some of Odysseus's comrades, sent to explore the island, stumbled upon Circe's palace, witnessing her seated regally amidst a retinue of transformed men-turned-animals. Employing her enchantments, Circe swiftly turned the warriors into swine with a mere wave of her staff.

    With the intervention of the god Hermes, Odysseus imbibed a protective herb, rendering him immune to Circe's spells. Foiled in her attempts, Circe found her magic ineffectual against Odysseus. Yielding to his demand for the restoration of his men, Circe consented, but only under the condition that Odysseus share her bedchamber. Despite his reservations, Odysseus agreed, leading to a year-long stay for him and his crew on Aeaea. At the end of that year, Odysseus decided to depart and resume his journey homeward. Circe, gifted with foresight, gave guidance to Odysseus, directing him to venture to the Underworld to seek counsel from the blind prophet Tiresias.

  • No living soul had dared enter the Underworld. But Odysseus, undeterred, ventured forth to continue his journey home to Ithaca. Along the River Acheron, he and his men made offerings to Hades. Odysseus then decided to take the dark path alone. There, the blind prophet Tiresias appeared, advising him to navigate between Scylla and Charybdis to reach his destination.

  • As they left Hades, Odysseus and his companions embarked on a voyage that stretched across many days, their eyes scanning the horizon in search of solid ground. Before long, however, an unsettling melody began to permeate the air, reaching the ears of those aboard the vessel. This haunting symphony stirred their souls, evoking both joy and sorrow. Odysseus swiftly recognized the telltale signsâ€”they were drawing near the Sirens, a peril Circe had warned him about.

    The enchantress had instructed him to seal the ears of his crew with wax, for any who succumbed to the Sirens' song would inevitably hurl themselves into the sea, lured to their demise by the winged creatures. Adhering to Circe's counsel, Odysseus diligently attended to the task, safeguarding his men from the seductive calls. However, his own curiosity beckoned him to experience the allure of the Sirens' melody. Thus, he commanded his sailors to tie him tightly to the mast with rope, ensuring he remained steadfast and unable to succumb to temptation.

    As the ship sailed closer, enveloped in silence by the wax, Odysseus found himself wrestling against his restraints, compelled to break free and plunge into the deep blue depths towards the mesmerizing song. Yet, the ropes held firm, preventing his escape. Meanwhile, his comrades, oblivious to both the Sirens' enchantment and their captain's struggle, pressed on, guided solely by the winds and the pull of the tide. Gradually, the melody of the Sirens waned as the ship sailed farther from the treacherous shore, leaving behind the allure of their song to fade into the distant horizon.

  • Following the word of Tiresius, Odysseus opted for the path that brought him close to Scylla, a six-headed monster once a woman, while on the other side lurked Charybdis, a ferocious whirlpool. Tiresius had advised Odysseus to offer six men to Scylla to ensure passage without further losses.

    Approaching the strait's mouth between Scylla and Charybdis, the warriors trembled with fear as violent deaths awaited on both sides. Only Odysseus remained silent, but saddened by the prospect of sacrificing six brave warriors, yet resolved to do so to safeguard the others. As they sailed past Scylla, she seized six men, sparing the rest. The echoes of the sacrificed men's screams haunted Odysseus, and he lamented his decision until his dying days, having kept his motive secret from his crew. Eventually, his ship navigated past Charybdis and survived.

  • Upon reaching the island of Thrinacia, Odysseus, exhausted and weary, commanded his ship to anchor. This island held sacred significance to Helios, the sun god, whose cattle roamed freely upon its grounds. Despite explicit warnings from Tiresius and Circe to refrain from harming the sacred beasts, Odysseus's men disobeyed his orders, indulging in slaughtering and feasting upon the cattle.

    Helios swiftly lodged a complaint with Zeus, vowing retribution by threatening to plunge the sun into Hades, never to rise again. Responding to Helios's grievance, Zeus threw a thunderbolt that sank Odysseus's ship as it departed from Thrinacia, annihilating every man aboard except for their courageous leader. Amidst the chaos, Odysseus found himself adrift, miraculously escaping the clutches of Scylla and Charybdis, eventually being cast ashore onto an unfamiliar island.

  • The island where Odysseus found himself was Ogygia. During this time, he dwelled with the nymph Calypso, who discovered him unconscious on the shore. She offered him eternal life in exchange for his love, yet Odysseus longed for his homeland, Ithaca, and his family: his unfortunate wife and his son, who had surely grown in his absence.

    Despite Calypso's beauty and power, she couldn't ease the yearning within Odysseus's heart. His sense of unfulfillment persisted. Nonetheless, Calypso had fallen deeply in love with him and was unwilling to release him. Acting on Zeus's command, Hermes intervened, instructing Calypso to let Odysseus depart. Eventually, Odysseus embarked on a raft he constructed himself, setting sail for Ithaca with only a makeshift vessel. Yet, once again, he found himself ensnared by a storm, washed ashore on yet another unfamiliar land.

  • Telemachus, the twenty-year-old son of Odysseus, embarked on a journey to find his long-lost father. His mother faced her own challenges, besieged by persistent suitors seeking her hand in marriage. Despite ten years passing since the end of the Trojan War, Odysseus had not returned, leaving Penelope in a constant state of uncertainty.

    Penelope devised a clever way to deter the suitors, claiming she was weaving a burial shroud for Odysseus' father. She insisted she would only consider marriage once the shroud was complete. By day, she wove the cloth, only to unravel her work at night, prolonging the suitors' wait indefinitely in hopes of Odysseus' return. However, her trick was betrayed by her maid, getting the suitors to renew their advances, demanding both Penelope's hand and rulership of Ithaca.

    Aware of his mother's successful evasion of the suitors, Telemachus embarked on his quest. With the guidance of the goddess Athena and accompanied by loyal warriors, he journeyed to Sparta to seek information from Menelaus, hoping for news of his father. Despite his efforts, Menelaus could provide no information, leading Telemachus to return to Ithaca disheartened.

  • The land of Phaeacians believed by historians to correspond to modern Corfu, served as the setting where Odysseus found refuge following the treacherous storm. Encountering Odysseus in a state of exhaustion, naked upon the shore, Princess Nafsica took him under her care and escorted him to the palace of her father. Within the court of King Alcinous and Queen Arete, Odysseus was greeted by the bard Demodocus, who recounted tales of the Trojan War.

    Overwhelmed by grief upon hearing narratives of the war and the Trojan Horse, which he had devised, Odysseus succumbed to tears. Concerned bystanders gathered around him, asking his true identity and the impact of the stories. It was then that Odysseus revealed his true self and the long journey to return to Ithaca.

    Moved by his words, the Phaeacians outfitted him with their swiftest vessel and ample provisions, bidding him farewell as he embarked on his voyage. Thus, the hero at long last set foot on Ithacan soil, eager to reunite with his wife Penelope and son Telemachus, both of whom he had been separated from for two whole decades.

  • Upon reaching Ithaca, Odysseus' arrival went unnoticed. Disguised as a beggar, he approached the palace. His first encounters were with old servants and his son, Telemachus. They informed him about the suitors troubling Penelope. Remaining disguised, Odysseus then met his wife, who failed to recognize him.

    He recounted tales of his bravery in the Trojan War, bringing tears to her eyes. Penelope, composed, challenged the suitors to string Odysseus' bow and shoot an arrow through twelve axe-handles.

    The suitors competed eagerly, unaware of the task's impossibility, for the bow was so strong only Odysseus could string it. After many failed attemps of the suitors Odysseus effortlessly strung the bow and shot an arrow through all twelve handles, causing chaos.

    Revealing his identity, Odysseus, aided by Telemachus and the swineherd Eumaeus, slaughtered the suitors and their maid-servants. Penelope, upon witnessing the massacre, remained skeptical of the beggar's claim.

    In front of Odysseus, Penelope ordered the palace servants to remove the bed from her bed-chamber to the hall outside. On hearing this, Odysseus bristled with anger and opposed the idea, saying that this bed had been fashioned out of a living oak by his own hand and none, save a god, none in the whole world could move it. Joyful, Penelope rushed to Odysseus and hugged him, with big tears in her eyes, for she was reassured that this man was her beloved husband returned to her. Only Odysseus knew the secret about their bed and his words were the proof she needed to believe him.
